About the Sullivan Time Bank

Helping others is like money in the bank, thanks to an innovative currency system introduced by the Sullivan Foundation. Time banking, created by Edgar S. Cahn in 1980, is a mode of exchange that allows people to swap their skills and time, rather than money, while helping fulfill unmet social needs. Participants earn and spend “time credits”—for every hour they spend on an activity that helps others, they receive one time credit. When they need help from other time bank members with particular skills, they can “spend” their time credits.

These tools encourage innovation and growth of local economies, while fostering civic engagement. Bridging divides and propelling progress, these service-based interactions give new life and meaning to the local economy.

How the Time Bank Benefits Your Community

With the isolation ensuing from Covid-19, partnered with a technologically-driven society, we are increasingly disconnected from the gift of community. During a time of isolation, we rediscovered the importance of daily interactions. Transitioning out of a pandemic and back into our neighborhoods, the Time Bank provides a way to foster intentional connection and strengthen bonds with strangers.

In addition to relationships, practical needs are met through the Time Bank, as lawns are mowed and walls are painted. This allows community members to sharpen their crafts, and maybe even rediscover some hobbies. Our credit system also allows those recovering from financial hardship a new way to move forward.
